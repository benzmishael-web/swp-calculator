Lesson 1 of 4

Your savings can pay you every month

Most of us think of savings as money we store — locked away for a rainy day. But what if your savings could pay you a salary every single month, without you having to work for it?

That is exactly what a Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP) does. You invest a lump sum of money into a mutual fund. Every month, a fixed amount is automatically transferred to your bank account. The remaining money stays invested and continues to grow.

How it works in simple terms

You invest a lump sum — say, 50 Lakh — into a mutual fund. Each month, the fund automatically transfers a fixed amount, say 30,000, into your bank account. The remaining 49 Lakh and 70,000 stays invested and earns returns. This continues month after month. The key question is: how long before the money runs out? That depends on how much you withdraw versus how much the investment earns. This calculator answers exactly that question.

What exactly is a mutual fund?

A mutual fund is a pool of money collected from many investors. A professional fund manager invests this pool into stocks, bonds, and other assets. Your money grows because these investments generate returns over time.

You do not need to be an expert to invest in a mutual fund. You simply put in money, and a team of professionals manages it for you. When you want your money back, you can withdraw it — that is where SWP comes in.

Term 01
Corpus
The total amount of money you invest. If you invest 50 Lakh, that is your corpus. It is the starting balance of your SWP.
Term 02
Return Rate
How fast your invested money grows each year, shown as a percentage. For example, 8% means 100 becomes 108 in one year.
Term 03
Inflation
The rate at which prices rise over time. If inflation is 5%, something costing 1,000 today will cost 1,050 next year.
Term 04
Withdrawal
The fixed amount you take out of your corpus each month. Think of it as your monthly income from savings.
Lesson 3 of 4

How does the calculation work?

The calculator uses a standard financial formula to figure out how your corpus changes every month. At the start of each month, your corpus earns interest. Then your withdrawal is deducted. What remains becomes next month's starting balance.

1
Your corpus earns interest this month
The remaining balance grows at the assumed annual return rate, divided by 12 for a monthly rate. For example, 8% per year becomes 0.67% per month.
2
Your withdrawal is deducted
The fixed monthly amount you chose is subtracted from the corpus. This is the money transferred to your bank account.
3
The remaining balance carries forward
Whatever is left becomes the opening balance for the next month. This process repeats every month until the corpus runs out or you stop.
4
The result: how many months your money lasts
The calculator counts how many months pass before the balance reaches zero — or tells you if the corpus is self-sustaining forever because the interest exceeds the withdrawal.
The Formula Used in This Calculator
Balance(n) = Balance(n-1) × (1 + r) − W
r = Annual Return / 12 W = Monthly Withdrawal n = Month number

Lesson 4 of 4

Three things that decide how long your money lasts

Whether your corpus lasts 10 years or 40 years depends on three factors. Understanding these will help you make better decisions with the calculator.

Factor 1
Withdrawal vs. Growth
If you withdraw more than your corpus earns in interest, your balance shrinks. If you withdraw less, it can last indefinitely. This ratio is the most important factor.
Factor 2
Inflation Step-Up
As prices rise, you will naturally need more money each month. A step-up withdrawal means your monthly amount increases by a fixed percentage every year to keep up with inflation.
Factor 3
Starting Corpus Size
A larger starting corpus gives you more runway. Doubling your corpus while keeping the same withdrawal roughly doubles how long your money lasts.
The Safe Withdrawal Rate — a useful benchmark

Financial planners generally suggest that withdrawing less than 4% of your corpus per year — which works out to roughly 0.33% per month — gives you the best chance of never running out of money. This is because long-term investment returns in diversified funds have historically exceeded this level. It is not a guarantee, but it is a widely used starting point for planning. This calculator will show you your own withdrawal rate so you can see exactly where you stand.
